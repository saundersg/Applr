yTe <- predict(truelm, newdata=mytest)

ybar <- mean(mytest$SalePrice) #Yi is given by Ynew from the new sample of data

# Compute SSTO
SSTO <- sum((mytest$SalePrice - ybar)^2)

# Compute SSE for each model using y - yhat
SSES <- sum((mytest$SalePrice - yTe)^2)

# Compute R-squared for each
rsS <- 1 - SSES/SSTO

# Compute adjusted R-squared for each
n <- length(mytest$SalePrice) # sample size
ps <- length(coef(truelm)) # num. parameters in model
rsaS <- 1 - (n-1)/(n-ps)*SSES/SSTO

Looking at the R-squared values, the model does a pretty good job explaining the variation in SalePrice. The Original R is 0.7718, which means about 77% of the differences in home prices can be explained by TotalSF, GarageCars, and KitchenAbvGr in the training data. The Adjusted R is just a little lower at 0.7706, which shows that the model is a good fit without adding unnecessary complexity. When tested on new data, the Validation R drops to 0.6625, meaning it still explains about 66% of the variation in home prices, but not quite as well as it did on the training data. The Validation Adjusted R is 0.6588, following the same pattern. This drop makes sense since models usually perform better on the data they were trained on. While the model still does a solid job predicting home prices, there might be other factors influencing SalePrice that arent included here. It could be worth looking into additional variables or interactions to see if the validation score improves.

The graph does a great job showing how Total Square Footage, GarageCars, and KitchenAbvGr influence the predicted SalePrice. Right away, I can see that as the total square footage of a home increases, so does the predicted price. That makes sensebigger homes usually sell for more. The trend is easy to follow, with the lines moving steadily upward, reinforcing the idea that home size is one of the biggest factors in price.

Garage space also plays a big role. The different colored lines represent different numbers of garage spaces, and theres a clear separation between them. Homes with more garage spaces tend to have higher predicted prices at every square footage level. The red line, representing homes with no garage, is consistently lower than the others, while the purple line, representing homes with four garages, is the highest. This makes it obvious that garage spaces are a major factor in home value.

KitchenAbvGr, or the number of above-grade kitchens, is also represented in the graph, but its effect isnt as dramatic as garage space. The different linetypes show how prices change based on the number of kitchens. The lines follow the same general upward trend, but they dont seem to spread apart as much as the GarageCars lines. This suggests that while having more kitchens does increase a homes value, it doesnt have as strong of an effect as garage space.

Looking at the data points, I can tell that the model fits the data pretty well. The dots, which represent actual SalePrice values, mostly follow along the prediction lines. There are some outliers here and there, which means there might be other factors affecting home prices that arent included in this model. But overall, the model does a solid job of predicting sale prices based on these three variables.
